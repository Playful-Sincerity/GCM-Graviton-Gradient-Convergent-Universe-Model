## The Claim

For the Bohr ground-state energy $E_1 = -\dfrac{m_e k^2 e^4}{2\hbar^2}$ to remain invariant under simultaneous perturbations $\Delta e$ and $\Delta m_e$, the perturbations must satisfy:

$$\frac{\Delta m_e}{m_e} = -4 \cdot \frac{\Delta e}{e}$$

This is a **consistency constraint on any emergent-charge-and-mass theory**: if electron charge and electron mass are both derived from a deeper substrate, their fluctuations must co-vary with a 1 : −4 ratio to preserve hydrogen spectroscopy (measured to parts per billion).

## Framing — Consistency Constraint, Not Prediction

E7 is **not** a prediction that $\Delta e \neq 0$ or $\Delta m_e \neq 0$. It says: *if* $e$ and $m_e$ both emerge from graviton density structure (as GCM claims), *then* their emergent values cannot vary independently. Any consistent emergent-parameter theory must satisfy this ratio.

## Worked Example

For the paper's illustrative case $\Delta e = 10^{-35}$ C:

$$\Delta m_e = -4 \cdot m_e \cdot \frac{\Delta e}{e} = -4 \cdot 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \cdot \frac{10^{-35}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx -2.274 \times 10^{-46} \text{ kg}$$

Consistent with the session brief's $\approx -2.27 \times 10^{-46}$ kg (0.2% agreement, within rounding).

## What This Claim Is

A derivation from a single standard non-relativistic quantum-mechanical formula (the Bohr ground state) showing that the two dimensional parameters entering it — $e$ and $m_e$ — cannot be independently varied if the spectrum is to be preserved. It is a *compatibility test* that GCM (or any emergent-parameter theory) must pass, not a new physical effect.

## What This Claim Is Not

- Not a claim that electron charge or mass drift over time. No such drift has been measured to the precision where $\Delta e \sim 10^{-35}$ C would matter.
- Not a claim that QED corrections are negligible. At the precision where this ratio becomes empirically testable, QED dominates the Bohr result (the Lamb shift and hyperfine structure introduce additional dependences).
- Not a relativistic result. The Bohr formula is non-relativistic; a Dirac-level or QED-level treatment would yield a different ratio.
