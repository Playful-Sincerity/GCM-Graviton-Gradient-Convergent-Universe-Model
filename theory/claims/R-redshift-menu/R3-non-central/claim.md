# R3 — Non-Central Position

**Claim code:** R3
**Category:** Cosmological Redshift Mechanism
**Session:** G (2026-04-23)
**Epistemic tier:** 🔴 Red
**R6 companion required:** Partially — gravitational redshift naturally produces time dilation

---

## The Claim

We are not at the gravitational center of the universe. The universe is converging toward a center of graviton density — a center that is elsewhere. Light emitted from distant sources is preferentially pulled toward this center rather than toward us. What arrives at us has traversed a gravitational potential gradient and lost energy, producing a cosmological gravitational redshift.

In this picture, redshift is not about photon-energy-loss-per-unit-distance (R1) or wavelength-stretching-in-voids (R2). It is a bulk gravitational effect: sources at cosmological distance sit in a slightly different gravitational potential than we do, and their light is redshifted by the potential difference on arrival.

## Proposed Structural Form

Gravitational redshift for a photon escaping a potential well of depth $\Delta\Phi$:

$$\frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda} \approx \frac{\Delta\Phi}{c^2}$$

For R3 to produce the observed Hubble relation, the cosmological potential gradient must be:

$$\frac{d\Phi}{dr} \sim c \cdot H_0 \approx 6.8 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m/s}^2$$

evaluated at the Hubble scale ($H_0 = 70$ km/s/Mpc). This is a specific, measurable anisotropy in the gravitational potential of the observable universe. Notably, this value is within a factor ~5 of the MOND acceleration scale $a_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-10}$ m/s² — a coincidence worth flagging (see `plausibility.md` for discussion).

## What Would Make R3 Attractive

Unlike R1, R3 does not need an R6 companion for time dilation. Gravitational redshift *automatically* produces time dilation — clocks in a deeper potential tick slower (GR equivalence principle). A photon traversing a cosmological potential gradient arrives with both reduced energy AND stretched time intervals, giving $(1+z)$ time dilation naturally. This is how gravitational redshift solves the SNe Ia time-dilation problem "for free."

## What Makes R3 Hard

1. **Requires a specific gravitational anisotropy.** The cosmological potential gradient must point in a specific direction and have a specific magnitude. This has not been measured in any direction. The observed universe is isotropic to very high precision on large scales (CMB anisotropies at the $10^{-5}$ level, after subtracting the dipole attributed to our peculiar velocity).
2. **Tension with CMB isotropy.** If we sit non-centrally in a converging universe, the CMB should show a gravitational dipole on top of the kinematic one. Interpretation of the observed CMB dipole (3.36 mK, attributed to our ~369 km/s motion relative to the CMB rest frame) would need revision — part of it would be gravitational, part kinematic.
3. **No quantitative model.** There is no specification of where the convergence center is, how far we are from it, or what the potential structure looks like. The claim is structural; it has not been quantified.

## Relation to Other Mechanisms

R3 is dimensionally distinct from R1, R2, R4, R5. It is the only mechanism that produces redshift as a bulk gravitational effect rather than a photon-propagation effect. This means R3 can coexist with R2 or R5 in an amalgamation — different fractions of observed redshift could come from different mechanisms — but R3 alone must explain the full $(1+z)$ relation.

## Honest Framing

R3 is included in the menu because it is a coherent candidate that arises naturally from GCM's convergence claim. It is marked 🔴 Red because:
- No measurement of the required cosmological potential anisotropy exists.
- Tension with CMB isotropy is real.
- No quantitative GCM model of the convergence structure has been built.

R3 may become yellow if: (a) a GCM-derived estimate of the current universe's convergence structure produces a potential anisotropy in a specific direction that can be searched for observationally, or (b) reanalysis of the CMB dipole decomposition allows for a gravitational component.
