# R5 — Selection Effect

**Claim code:** R5
**Category:** Cosmological Redshift Mechanism
**Session:** G (2026-04-23)
**Epistemic tier:** 🟡 Yellow
**R6 companion required:** Yes (selection effects do not stretch time on their own)
**Origin:** Wisdom's 2026-04-23 intuition — *"light doesn't reach us because we're small"*

---

## The Claim

What we observe as cosmological redshift is, in part, a *selection effect*: we sample a biased subset of all emitted light. At greater distances, the fraction of emitted photons that reach us becomes smaller and smaller, and the photons that *do* reach us are systematically the low-energy tail of the emission distribution.

This is structurally distinct from the other mechanisms:

- **R1, R2, R4** are claims about what happens to photons *in transit*. They posit a physical transformation of the photon as it travels.
- **R3** is a claim about the gravitational potential at the observer's location.
- **R5** is a claim about *which photons reach us*. It makes no assertion about what happens to individual photons in transit.

In R5, an individual photon emitted by a distant source *either reaches us with essentially its original properties, or doesn't reach us at all*. There is no energy-loss-in-transit claim. The redshift we observe is a statistical artifact of sampling.

## Why This Avoids Tired-Light Pitfalls

Classical tired light is falsified because it predicts zero time dilation in SNe Ia light curves. The falsification depends on tired light being a claim about *individual photon physics* — that photons lose energy in transit.

R5 makes no such claim. If a selection effect biases the distribution of photons we receive, the individual photons we do receive retain their original properties. The DES 2024 time-dilation measurement tests a property of individual SNe Ia light curves (how long they take to rise and fall), and under R5 that property is unaffected by the selection bias on emission energy.

**But this is also R5's central limitation.** A selection effect explains why we see fewer or lower-energy photons from distant sources; it does not explain why SNe Ia light curves take $(1+z)$ longer to unfold. The $(1+z)^{1.003 \pm 0.005}$ time-dilation measurement is *not* a measurement of energy; it is a measurement of temporal stretching. Selection effects do not produce temporal stretching.

So R5 needs an additional mechanism to explain SNe Ia time dilation — R6 (photon-structure geometric time dilation), or a separate story about why distant sources' intrinsic timescales appear slowed.

## Wisdom's "We're Small" Framing

The intuition R5 encodes: we are one small observer in a vast universe. The light that reaches our small detector area from a distant source is a tiny fraction of the light that source emits. That fraction is biased by whatever physics determines which photons travel in our direction and which don't. If the bias favors lower-energy photons (because high-energy photons are more likely to interact with intervening matter, or because emission at specific angles favors specific energies), then the light we receive is systematically low-energy relative to the source's full spectrum.

This is a testable intuition in principle, though difficult in practice. It would predict that statistical inhomogeneities in the observed-photon distribution (from a given source, over a given aperture) reflect the physics of emission-to-detection path selection, not of in-transit photon transformation.

## Possible Microscopic Mechanisms

R5 does not commit to a specific microscopic mechanism. Candidates include:

1. **Gravitational lensing bias.** Photons emitted at energies that interact more strongly with intervening gravitational structure are preferentially deflected out of our line of sight.
2. **Absorption-scattering bias.** Intervening matter (including PBHs, cold dust, warm-hot intergalactic medium) absorbs and re-emits photons with an energy-dependent cross-section, preferentially removing high-energy photons from the line of sight.
3. **Geometric aperture bias.** We are a small target; the solid angle we subtend at a distant source is tiny, and the photons hitting this solid angle are a biased sample from the source's full emission.

None of these is specified in detail in GCM. R5 names the *type* of effect rather than its specific realization.

## What This Is Not

R5 is not the observational selection bias known as *Malmquist bias* — that is a selection effect on which *sources* we see (bright sources over-represented at large distances). R5 is a selection effect on *which photons from a source* reach us.

R5 is not the statement "we happen to be positioned in a special region" (that's R3). R5 is a claim about the emission-to-detection filter.

## Honest Framing

R5 is Wisdom's original intuition and it has structural merit: it encodes the "what arrives at the observer isn't necessarily representative of what was emitted" epistemic move, which is often overlooked in standard cosmological reasoning. Yellow tier reflects:

- Clean on ISW (no void-growth requirement).
- Avoids tired-light pitfall on photon physics.
- Structurally distinct from R1–R4 in a genuine way.

But also:
- Cannot produce SNe Ia time dilation — the $(1+z)^{1.003}$ measurement is on temporal structure, not energy.
- Needs R6 companion or a separate time-dilation explanation.
- No specific microscopic mechanism specified.
- Difficult to falsify until a specific mechanism is proposed.
