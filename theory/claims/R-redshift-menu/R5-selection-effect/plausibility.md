# R5 — Plausibility

## The Epistemic Structure

R5 is the only mechanism in the menu that operates at the epistemological level rather than the photon-physics level. Its central move:

> "We don't know what the emitted light distribution looks like; we only know what we receive. The systematic low-energy bias of what we receive is a measurement of the emission-to-detection filter, not a measurement of what happens to photons in transit."

This is a genuinely different epistemic claim. It doesn't contradict or replace R1–R4 so much as reframe what the observed redshift-distance relation tells us. In a pure R5 world:

- Photons don't lose energy in transit.
- The distribution of what we detect is biased toward low energy.
- The observed $z$-distance relation measures how this bias scales with distance, not how photon energies decay over distance.

## Why This Is Attractive

**(1) ISW-clean.** R5 does not require void growth or evolving potentials. It is compatible with a quasi-static universe where the graviton density structure changes slowly on the timescale of photon transit. This avoids R2's central tension.

**(2) Blackbody-compatible.** If R5 is a selection effect on emission direction / path / intervening-matter interactions, it can be frequency-independent at the level needed. High-energy and low-energy photons can be filtered with the same bias if the filter is a geometric / cross-section effect that averages out frequency dependence.

**(3) Consistent with GCM's "matter we can't see" position.** In GCM, D1 claims that inferred dark matter is ordinary matter whose light doesn't reach us. R5 makes the same structural move for redshift: observed low-energy bias reflects which photons reach us, not what happens to them. These two positions are sibling applications of the same epistemic principle.

**(4) Wisdom's "we're small" has teeth.** The emphasis on our smallness as observers is not a metaphor — it is a real statement about the tiny solid angle we subtend at cosmological distances, and the consequent extreme selection on which photons hit our detectors. This is physics, not vibes.

## The Time-Dilation Problem (Explicit)

R5's biggest specific vulnerability: selection effects do not stretch time.

The DES 2024 SNe Ia result ($(1+z)^{1.003 \pm 0.005}$) is a measurement of temporal structure. A SNe Ia at $z = 1$ takes approximately twice as long to rise and fall, from our perspective, as the same SNe Ia at $z = 0$. This is not a statement about photon energy — it is a statement about how long the light curve takes to unfold.

Selection effects on which photons reach us cannot produce this temporal stretching. If we filter the emitted photon distribution to keep only low-energy photons, the resulting light curve has the same temporal structure as the original, just at a different spectral peak. Time is not stretched.

**Three possible repairs:**

**(A) R5 + R6.** Pair R5 with R6 (photon-structure geometric time dilation). R5 handles the energy-distribution aspect of redshift; R6 handles the time-dilation aspect. But this makes R5 + R6 structurally overlap with R1 + R6 (friction + geometric time dilation) in that both end up with "redshift = energy shift + separate time-dilation mechanism."

**(B) R5 partial.** R5 accounts for *some* fraction of observed redshift; the remainder (including the time-dilation part) comes from R2 or another in-transit mechanism. In this reading, R5 is a partial mechanism, not a complete one.

**(C) R5 + emission-time-rate bias.** If the selection effect includes a bias toward emissions at particular source-frame time-rates (unlikely, but conceivable), there could be a time-structural aspect to the bias. This is speculative.

**(A) is the cleanest.** R5 + R6 is a valid menu item. But it is worth explicitly acknowledging that R5 by itself cannot touch the SNe Ia constraint — it needs R6 (or timescape-like mechanism, or R3's automatic-GR time dilation) as a companion.

## Order-of-Magnitude Sketch

If the selection fraction of photons reaching our detector from a source at distance $d$ scales as $f(d) \propto e^{-\beta d}$, and if the bias on the received energy distribution is $\langle E \rangle_{\text{received}} / \langle E \rangle_{\text{emitted}} \propto e^{-\gamma d}$, then the effective "redshift" is:

$$1 + z = \frac{\langle E \rangle_{\text{emitted}}}{\langle E \rangle_{\text{received}}} \sim e^{\gamma d}$$

For $z = 1$ at $d \sim $ Hubble distance, $\gamma \sim H_0/c \approx 2.3 \times 10^{-27}$ m$^{-1}$. This is the same order as R1's $\alpha$, by construction — any mechanism reproducing Hubble's law at low $z$ will have this scale.

**Constraint: $\gamma$ must be frequency-independent to satisfy CMB blackbody.** If the selection bias is frequency-dependent, blackbody is distorted.

## Where Plausibility Falls Off

- **No specific microscopic mechanism.** Gravitational-lensing bias, absorption-scattering bias, geometric aperture bias are named but not developed.
- **Frequency-independence is not obvious.** Most absorption-scattering processes are frequency-dependent.
- **Empirical distinction from in-transit mechanisms is hard.** Within current observational precision, R5 and R2 may be indistinguishable on standard Hubble-diagram measurements. Distinguishing them requires searching for specific predictions (e.g., R5 would predict an angular-correlation signature in the Hubble diagram from intervening matter; R2 would not).

## Honest Framing

R5 is a real mechanism — the epistemic move it encodes is valid, and the ISW-clean and blackbody-compatible features are genuine advantages over R2. But R5 is yellow rather than green because:

- The microscopic mechanism is unspecified.
- The time-dilation companion is required.
- The mechanism is hard to empirically distinguish from in-transit alternatives without additional specification.

This mechanism deserves development, not dismissal. It is Wisdom's intuition and it may prove the most honest mechanism on the menu if its development succeeds.
