## The Claim

There is no new particle beyond the Standard Model constituting dark matter. What is inferred as "dark matter" (from galactic rotation curves, gravitational lensing, CMB power spectrum, and large-scale structure) is *ordinary matter whose electromagnetic radiation does not reach us* — due to absorption, scattering, gravitational capture, or intrinsic darkness (objects that emit little to no light of their own).

Primordial black holes (PBHs) in the asteroid-mass window ($10^{17}$ to $10^{23}$ g) are a specific candidate form of this ordinary invisible matter. They emit negligible detectable radiation at any plausible observer distance (Hawking $T_H \approx 10^6$ K at $10^{17}$ kg, but Schwarzschild radius $\sim 10^{-10}$ m gives total Hawking luminosity $\sim 36$ mW per PBH — undetectable at cosmic distances), they are not constrained by current microlensing surveys (the window is the least-constrained PBH region), and they are general-relativistic objects — no new physics required.

## The GCM Framing

In GCM's ontology, everything is gravitons. "Ordinary matter" and "gravitons making up ordinary-matter-like structures" are the same substance. There is no special "dark matter" substance in GCM; there is only graviton density configured in various ways. Some configurations emit EM radiation we can detect (stars, hot gas, ionized plasma); others don't (cold compact objects, PBHs, rogue planets, brown dwarfs, cold molecular clouds of baryonic matter).

D1 is the application of this ontological commitment to the observational dark-matter question: the observed "dark matter" is not a new kind of thing, it is existing kinds of things we don't see.

## Quantitative Positioning

**Milky Way dark matter halo:** inferred total mass $M_{\text{halo}} \sim 10^{12} M_{\odot}$; visible baryonic mass $\sim 6 \times 10^{10} M_{\odot}$; ratio $\sim 17$. Roughly 94% of the halo mass is "dark."

**If all DM is asteroid-mass PBHs at $\sim 10^{20}$ g $= 10^{-13} M_{\odot}$:**

$$N_{\text{PBH}} = \frac{M_{\text{halo, dark}}}{M_{\text{PBH}}} \sim \frac{10^{12} M_{\odot}}{10^{-13} M_{\odot}} = 10^{25} \text{ PBHs in the Milky Way halo}$$

This number is enormous but not observationally excluded:

- **Microlensing:** LMC/SMC microlensing surveys (EROS, MACHO, OGLE, HSC/Subaru) constrain PBH fractions in the $10^{-7}$ to $10^{12} M_{\odot}$ range. The asteroid-mass window ($10^{17}$–$10^{23}$ g $\approx 10^{-16}$ to $10^{-10} M_{\odot}$) is below the sensitivity of most optical surveys, which are sensitive to Einstein-ring crossings on timescales of hours to months. Asteroid-mass PBHs produce microlensing on sub-second to sub-minute timescales, requiring dedicated high-cadence searches.
- **Hawking radiation:** Hawking temperature for a $10^{20}$ g = $10^{17}$ kg PBH is $T_H \approx 1.23 \times 10^6$ K — actually much *hotter* than the CMB, not colder. But the Schwarzschild radius is only $\sim 1.5 \times 10^{-10}$ m and the emitting surface area $\sim 3 \times 10^{-19}$ m², so total Hawking luminosity is $\sim 36$ mW per PBH. Undetectable at cosmic distances because of negligible power, not because of low temperature. (Solar-mass PBH calibration $T_H(M_\odot) = 6.17 \times 10^{-8}$ K matches textbook, confirming formula.)
- **Femtolensing / nanolensing:** some upper bounds on asteroid-mass PBHs from gamma-ray burst lensing (Barnacka et al., 2012). Recent analyses (2023) tighten but don't close the window.
- **Stellar dynamics:** PBHs in this mass range are too small to disrupt star clusters or globular clusters.

**The asteroid-mass window is the least-constrained region** of PBH parameter space and could support $f_{\text{PBH}} = 1$ (all DM is PBH) at present observational bounds, per Profumo group's 2019–2025 work.

## Alternative Invisible Ordinary Matter

D1 is not committed to PBHs specifically. Other candidates for "matter whose light doesn't reach us":

- **Cold compact baryonic objects:** brown dwarfs, rogue planets, cold white dwarfs, cold neutron stars. Traditionally constrained by MACHO searches, but at low masses these searches have gaps.
- **Cold molecular gas:** very cold H$_2$ with no dust emission is hard to detect. Pfenniger & Combes (1994) and follow-up work suggest this could hide significant baryonic mass.
- **Warm-hot intergalactic medium:** known to exist, partially detected via X-ray absorption, but fraction of total mass still uncertain.
- **Absorbed / scattered light:** bright sources whose radiation is absorbed by intervening cold matter before reaching us.

D1 is the broad structural claim: there is *enough room* in invisible ordinary matter to account for observed DM effects without invoking new particles. PBHs are the most concrete candidate but not the only one.

## What D1 Does Not Yet Do

- **No specific halo density profile.** Standard NFW or Einasto halo profiles fit rotation curves well; D1 currently doesn't predict these profiles from first principles. A concrete D1 model would need to show why invisible ordinary matter has the observed spatial distribution.
- **No CMB power spectrum fit.** The CMB acoustic peaks at $\ell \sim 200, 500, 800$ depend on the dark-matter-to-baryon ratio at recombination. If D1's "dark matter" is ordinary baryonic matter, the observed CMB is inconsistent with baryon-only $\Lambda$CDM. D1 either needs the early universe to have a different physics than standard cosmology, or needs PBHs specifically to act as "cold non-baryonic matter" from the recombination perspective (which is plausible for PBHs since they don't couple to photons as baryons do).
- **No large-scale structure simulation.** N-body simulations of structure formation use particle dark matter. D1 would need simulations with PBH distributions (or other invisible-ordinary-matter distributions) to show structure forms correctly.

## Structural Relation to R5 (Selection Effect)

D1 and R5 share the same epistemic move at different scales:

- R5: the observed light distribution from a source is biased by which photons reach us; "redshift" is a selection artifact.
- D1: the observed matter distribution is biased by which matter emits detectable light; "dark matter" is a selection artifact.

Both are applications of the principle "what we observe is not necessarily what is." This coherence across R and D claims is one of GCM's appealing features — it is the same framework, not two different rescue hypotheses.

## Honest Flag

D1 is the structural claim. The *specific* claim that PBHs in the asteroid-mass window account for a significant fraction of DM is an empirically open question — Profumo's group continues to constrain the window, and any new observation could tighten it.
