# D1 — Empirical Check

Evaluation of D1 (no dark matter particle; inferred DM is ordinary invisible matter, including asteroid-mass PBHs) against observational dark-matter evidence.

## 1. Galactic Rotation Curves

**Observation:** Flat or slowly declining rotation curves at large radii from galactic centers — inconsistent with visible-baryon-only gravity, consistent with an extended halo.

**D1's response:** The halo is real; it is made of invisible ordinary matter (PBHs, cold compact objects, cold gas, etc.). The spatial distribution must be determined by the formation history of these invisible components.

**Status:** 🟡 Consistent at order-of-magnitude (enough room in invisible matter); specific halo profile from D1 components is not derived.

## 2. CMB Acoustic Peaks

**Observation:** Planck CMB power spectrum shows acoustic peaks at $\ell \sim 200, 500, 800$ whose amplitudes require $\Omega_{\text{DM}} h^2 \sim 0.12$ at recombination, distinct from baryon density $\Omega_b h^2 \sim 0.022$.

**D1's response:** At recombination, "dark matter" behaves as gravitationally-active, photon-decoupled matter. PBHs formed before recombination do exactly this: they are cold, don't couple to photons, gravitationally cluster. A PBH DM scenario (or other pre-recombination invisible matter) reproduces the CMB peaks.

**Status:** 🟢/🟡 PBH DM can in principle reproduce the CMB; detailed power-spectrum fits for PBH DM have been performed in the literature (Ali-Haïmoud 2019, Carr et al. 2021) and are consistent with observations in the asteroid-mass window.

## 3. Gravitational Lensing (Cluster-Scale)

**Observation:** Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-56) and similar systems show gravitational lensing displaced from the hot gas, consistent with most mass being collisionless.

**D1's response:** Asteroid-mass PBHs are effectively collisionless (cross-section-to-mass ratio is negligible). They would follow the dark-matter distribution in such systems, producing the observed lensing-gas displacement.

**Status:** 🟢 Consistent with collisionless-DM behavior.

## 4. Structure Formation / Large-Scale Structure

**Observation:** Galaxy correlation functions, filament networks, void statistics follow $\Lambda$CDM predictions with cold dark matter.

**D1's response:** Cold, collisionless, non-light-emitting PBHs behave gravitationally like CDM. Large-scale structure in a PBH-DM universe should reproduce observed statistics to leading order.

**Status:** 🟢/🟡 Gravitational behavior is correct; detailed N-body simulations with realistic PBH distributions are ongoing in the literature (Carr & Kuhnel 2020 review).

## 5. Microlensing Constraints on PBHs

**Observation:** EROS, MACHO, OGLE, HSC/Subaru exclude PBH DM across much of the mass spectrum, but the asteroid-mass window ($10^{17}$–$10^{23}$ g) remains open.

**D1's response:** D1 positions itself in this window (or in non-PBH invisible ordinary matter). Asteroid-mass PBHs are not excluded by current microlensing.

**Status:** 🟢 Compatible with current bounds.

## 6. Direct Detection Experiments (WIMP searches)

**Observation:** XENON-1T, XENONnT, LUX-ZEPLIN have ruled out WIMPs across most of the theoretically motivated mass-cross-section space.

**D1's response:** D1 explicitly does not posit a WIMP-like particle. Direct detection null results are consistent with D1.

**Status:** 🟢 Consistent. Direct detection null results support the "no new particle" claim.

## 7. Axion Searches

**Observation:** Axion searches (ADMX, HAYSTAC) have not produced detections in sensitive regions.

**D1's response:** Does not depend on axions.

**Status:** 🟢 Consistent.

## 8. Cosmic Dawn Anomalies (JWST)

**Observation:** JWST has found early massive black holes at $z \sim 8$–$10$, more massive than expected from standard stellar-collapse seeds.

**D1's response:** Profumo's 2025 work (Koulen, Profumo & Smyth, PRD) shows PBHs above $\sim 100 M_{\odot}$ can accelerate Population III star formation and seed these early massive BHs. This is supportive of the broader PBH program, and by extension of D1.

**Status:** 🟢 Supportive.

## 9. Rotation Curve Diversity

**Observation:** Rotation curves show substantial diversity at the low-mass (dwarf galaxy) scale — some galaxies fit $\Lambda$CDM predictions, others have "cusp-core" problems (central density profiles flatter than predicted).

**D1's response:** Diversity of rotation curves may reflect diversity of invisible-matter formation histories. Less homogeneous than a single DM particle species would produce. D1 provides no specific fit to dwarf-galaxy diversity, but the diversity is consistent with "invisible matter is a heterogeneous class," not with "invisible matter is one kind of thing."

**Status:** 🟡 Consistent in direction; not predictive in detail.

## The Verdict

D1 is empirically plausible. Every observational test that pins down dark matter is either neutral toward D1 or supportive. The remaining question is whether the detailed quantitative predictions of an invisible-ordinary-matter scenario (specifically, PBH DM in the asteroid-mass window) match all observations at precision — this is what Profumo's program continues to work on.

D1 is yellow because the full quantitative case runs through Profumo-style PBH-DM modeling, which is not GCM-specific. GCM contributes the structural claim; the Profumo program contributes the observational fit. Tying these together explicitly is strategic outreach work.
