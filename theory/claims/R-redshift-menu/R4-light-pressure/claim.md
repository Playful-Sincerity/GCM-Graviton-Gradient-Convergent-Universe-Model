# R4 — Light-Pressure Sensitivity

**Claim code:** R4
**Category:** Cosmological Redshift Mechanism
**Session:** G (2026-04-23)
**Epistemic tier:** 🔴 Red
**R6 companion required:** Likely yes — R4 alone does not obviously produce time dilation

---

## The Claim

A photon's frequency is sensitive to the ambient graviton "pressure" in the substrate it is currently passing through. In regions of lower graviton pressure (voids, underdense regions), the photon exhibits lower frequency; in regions of higher pressure (walls, dense regions), higher frequency. The observed photon frequency at the detector is set by the pressure at the *current* location of the photon, not by some transit-accumulated property.

Cosmological redshift emerges if the average line-of-sight path from a distant source traverses regions of systematically lower pressure than the region where the source emitted. Since voids dominate large-scale volume (~60–80% of cosmic volume), and since voids are lower-density than emission regions (stars, galaxies), distant photons preferentially sample low-pressure environments and arrive with lower frequency.

## Why This is Related to but Different from R2

R2 is about wavelength stretching during void transit — the mechanism operates *on* the photon as it traverses. R4 is about the photon's frequency being set by the *current ambient pressure* — the mechanism operates *at the observer*. These are epistemologically different:

- R2: the photon is modified by its journey.
- R4: the photon reads off the local pressure wherever it is.

In R4, if you moved a photon from a void into a dense region, its frequency would shift back up — there is no memory of its journey. In R2, once a photon has been redshifted by a void transit, it stays redshifted.

## Proposed Functional Form (No Derivation)

$$\nu_{\text{observed}} \propto \rho_g(\vec{r}_{\text{observer}})^k$$

for some exponent $k$. The sign and magnitude of $k$ would determine how sensitively photon frequency responds to ambient graviton density. This is a *placeholder* form — it is not derived from any underlying GCM dynamics.

## Why This is Red

R4 is included in the menu because it is conceptually distinguishable from the other mechanisms and has some intuitive appeal (photons "read" the local substrate). But as specified, R4 has **no quantitative form** for the pressure-frequency relation, and **no explanation** for why cosmological photons arrive redshifted rather than simply shifting as they pass through voids.

The core difficulty: if frequency is set by current ambient pressure, then photons from distant sources that are currently at our location should have the frequency appropriate to *our* local pressure, not the pressure of wherever they came from. Why would distant-source photons arrive with lower frequency than local-source photons, if both are at our location when measured?

One possible answer: the photon retains *some* memory of its emission conditions (internal structure that adjusts slowly). But this makes R4 structurally similar to R2 — a transit-accumulated effect — and loses its distinguishing feature.

## Relation to Other Mechanisms

R4 as a standalone mechanism is poorly defined. It might function as an additional effect *on top of* R1/R2/R5, producing residual frequency-dependent distortions beyond what those mechanisms predict. As a primary redshift mechanism, it lacks specification.

## Honest Framing

R4 is the weakest-specified mechanism in the menu. It is retained because:

1. The intuition (photons respond to local substrate) is real in GCM.
2. Light-matter interactions in any GCM-style theory will include pressure-like sensitivities.
3. A future refined version of R4 might resolve the mechanism ambiguity.

But at present, R4 is a concept without a mathematical body. Promotion to yellow requires a specific pressure-frequency relation derived from GCM dynamics, with a clear distinction from R2's wavelength-stretching mechanism.
