## What S5.5 Establishes

- **(a)** Newtonian gravity emerges as the large-separation, many-graviton limit of GCM's minimal-model equation. Full derivation with 4/4 verification checks. 🟢 Green.
- **(b)** Special relativity is consistent with GCM *if* gravitons propagate effects at a universal speed $c$. Robertson's (1949) derivation of Lorentz transformations from a substrate with universal signal speed provides the structural scaffold. 🟡 Yellow framing.
- **(c)** Local realism — when properly qualified as superdeterministic following 't Hooft (2016) — is a defensible minority position. The framing is clean for a Bell-aware physicist audience. 🟡 Yellow.

## What S5.5 Does NOT Establish

### General Relativity Not Addressed

S5.5(b) covers *special* relativity. General relativity — curved spacetime, stress-energy sourcing, gravitational wave dynamics — is a much deeper recovery problem and is not part of S5.5. It belongs to the semiclassical GR limit challenge in S8.

Open questions deliberately not touched in S5.5:
- How does GCM reproduce the Schwarzschild metric in the weak-field limit?
- How does GCM produce gravitational wave propagation at $c$ with the correct polarization structure (tensor modes, no scalar/vector modes)?
- How does GCM handle perihelion precession (the classic Mercury test)?
- How does GCM handle frame dragging?

All five would need to be addressed in a full GR-recovery sub-claim. That is a Session-H / S8-scope problem, not this session.

### Post-Newtonian Corrections Not Derived

S5.5(a) produces the leading-order Newtonian law. Post-Newtonian corrections (relativistic corrections to Kepler orbits, gravitational binding energy contributions, gravitational radiation reaction) are not derived. A full post-Newtonian expansion of GCM's substrate dynamics would be needed — and that work connects to the GR-recovery problem above.

### $c$ Not Derived from GCM Parameters (OQ-3)

The value of the speed of light is treated as an input to S5.5(b), not as an output of GCM dynamics. A complete theory would express $c$ as a function of $m_g$, graviton density, and the gravitational coupling — analogous to how $c^2 = 1/(\epsilon_0\mu_0)$ in Maxwell's theory. OQ-3 remains open.

### No Quantitative Bell Correlations

S5.5(c) commits to superdeterminism as the Bell escape but does not derive quantum correlations (e.g., $\cos^2(\theta/2)$ for entangled-spin measurements) from GCM dynamics. Doing so would require a full GCM theory of what quantum "states" are, how entanglement arises in the substrate, and how measurement interactions play out. All three are large open problems.

### Lorentz Invariance Tests Not Run

Modern Kennedy-Thorndike and other Lorentz-invariance tests are at $10^{-15}$ precision. S5.5(b) claims *structural* compatibility with Lorentz invariance, not precision-tested compatibility. Any future experimental detection of tiny Lorentz-violating effects would require GCM to address them. This is a future-work constraint, not a current gap.

### Free Will Framing Acknowledged as Philosophical Cost

S5.5(c) commits to superdeterminism, which philosophically denies a libertarian reading of free will. This is named in the document as a cost of the position, along with its resolution in GCM's ethos (converging universe, we-are-the-universe framing). Some readers will find this cost too high. The framing notes this honestly.

## How Each Sub-claim Becomes Green

- **(a) is already green.** No upgrade path needed; derivation is rigorous.
- **(b) becomes green when:** (i) OQ-3 is closed (derive $c$ from graviton parameters), (ii) a formal wave equation for substrate perturbations produces Lorentz-covariant propagation, (iii) $E = mc^2$ emerges as a derived consequence. Substantial theoretical project; expected beyond the coherence-paper horizon.
- **(c) becomes green when:** (i) GCM produces a quantitative prediction of Bell correlations, (ii) the measurement problem is solved in GCM terms (how does apparent wavefunction collapse emerge from graviton dynamics?). Extremely large open problem; not expected on any near-term horizon.
