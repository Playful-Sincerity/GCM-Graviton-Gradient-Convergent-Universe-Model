## Why This Document Exists

The phrase "local realism" is a red flag for physicists. Bell's theorem (1964) proves that no theory of local hidden variables can reproduce the full set of quantum-mechanical predictions — and experiment has confirmed quantum mechanics on this point (Aspect 1982; Hensen et al. 2015 loophole-free). Any program that claims "local realism" without qualification will be dismissed within seconds by anyone familiar with Bell's theorem.

GCM claims local realism. This document makes the qualification explicit: GCM's local realism is **superdeterministic**, following 't Hooft (2016). Without this qualifier, the claim is indefensible. With it, the claim is a minority position in foundations of physics — but a non-crackpot one, held by a Nobel laureate and defended by a serious researcher in philosophy of physics.

## The Two Commitments

### Commitment 1: Realism

GCM commits to **realism** about the graviton substrate. This means:

- Graviton density distributions are real physical structures — they have definite values at every point in space and time, independent of whether they are measured.
- What quantum mechanics describes as a "probability amplitude" or "wave function" is, in GCM's reading, a computational shorthand for a real density field: $\rho_g(\mathbf{x}, t)$ has a definite value at every $(\mathbf{x}, t)$, even when we don't know it.
- Wave-particle duality (T4) is **ontic**, not epistemic. The extended gradient structure of a particle is a real physical structure, not a reflection of measurement limitations. Apparent stochasticity in quantum measurement reflects ignorance of the exact graviton microstate — not fundamental indeterminism.

This is the "hidden variable realist" position in the foundations-of-physics literature. GCM's "hidden variables" are the positions and momenta of all gravitons, which are definite at all times but not in general accessible to measurement without disturbing the system.

### Commitment 2: Locality

GCM commits to **locality**: no faster-than-light signaling; no action at a distance beyond what contact (or propagation through a dense graviton substrate) permits.

The minimal model equation of motion — $\ddot{\mathbf{x}}_i = \sum_j G m_g / \max(r_{ij}, \ell_{\min})^2 \cdot \hat{r}_{ij}$ — is local in the sense that the force on graviton $i$ comes from the actual positions of other gravitons at the present time. More careful treatment (required in a relativistic extension) would invoke retarded positions — the positions at the time the force signal departed — which is the standard relativistic causality requirement.

**These two commitments together are called local realism.** And they are apparently ruled out by Bell's theorem.

## Bell's Theorem and the Standard Escape Routes

Bell's theorem (1964) proves: no theory of **local hidden variables** can reproduce all the statistical predictions of quantum mechanics, given the assumption of **measurement independence**.

Three escape routes from Bell:

| Escape | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Abandon locality | Nonlocal realism (Bohmian mechanics) | Hidden variables exist; they are nonlocal — the pilot wave is nonlocal. Used by some GCM-adjacent programs. |
| Abandon realism | Copenhagen / instrumentalism | No hidden variables; measurement outcomes are fundamental. Abandoned by GCM. |
| Abandon measurement independence | Superdeterminism | Measurement choices and particle states are not independent; their correlation is explained by common history in a deterministic universe. |

The 2026-04-03 GCM framework debate synthesis identified escape route 1 (nonlocal realism, Bohmian-type) as GCM's path. This has been **superseded** by Wisdom's 2026-04-23 commitment: GCM takes escape route 3. The reason: Bohmian mechanics accepts nonlocal signaling (the pilot wave acts instantaneously across arbitrary distances), which is in tension with T4's reading of space as graviton distances and T2's purely local graviton-to-graviton interaction. Locality is more naturally embedded in GCM's substrate picture than nonlocality is.

## Superdeterminism: The Argument

**What measurement independence says.** Bell's derivation assumes that an experimenter's choice of measurement settings $a, b$ (which detectors to align, which polarization to measure) is statistically independent of the particles' hidden variable state $\lambda$:

$$P(\lambda \mid a, b) = P(\lambda)$$

In ordinary language: the particle state $\lambda$ does not "know" in advance how the experimenter will choose to measure it.

**What superdeterminism denies.** In a fully deterministic universe, the experimenter's choice of $a, b$ is itself a physical event — the result of prior physical states of the experimenter's brain, the laboratory, the random-number generator, and all causal antecedents. The particle's hidden variable state $\lambda$ is likewise a result of prior physical states. In a deterministic universe, both are fixed at $t = 0$ by the same initial conditions.

Therefore: the correlation $P(\lambda \mid a, b) \neq P(\lambda)$ is not a signal between the measurement apparatus and the particle — it is a correlation due to **common history** in a deterministic flow. The correlation is not explaining anything by faster-than-light communication; it is explaining it by the fact that in a deterministic universe, every event is correlated with every other through the causal chain.

Bell-violating correlations in quantum experiments are, on this view, artifacts of the fact that "random" measurement choices are not truly random — they are deterministic outputs of physical processes that share causal history with the particle states. The correlations are not surprising; they are necessary consequences of the determinism.

**Formal statement of the denial:**

$$P(\lambda \mid a, b) \neq P(\lambda)\ \text{in general}$$

This single denial is all that superdeterminism requires. It does not require conspiracy, fine-tuning, or faster-than-light communication. It requires that the universe be deterministic — that $a$, $b$, and $\lambda$ all be outputs of the same initial-condition function.

## Precedent: 't Hooft's Cellular Automaton Interpretation

The most developed superdeterministic program is Gerard 't Hooft's *Cellular Automaton Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics* (Springer, 2016). 't Hooft (Nobel Prize, Physics, 1999) argues:

1. At the Planck scale, physics is governed by a discrete deterministic cellular automaton — each cell evolves by local rules with no randomness.
2. Quantum mechanics is not a fundamental theory but an effective description of this underlying deterministic system, valid in limits where the automaton's fine-grained correlations are not resolved.
3. Bell-violating correlations arise because the "random" choices of experimenters are not independent of the cellular automaton's state — they are part of the same deterministic flow.

GCM's graviton substrate is structurally analogous: a Planck-scale deterministic system (all graviton positions and velocities fixed at $t = 0$, evolving by the local equation of motion) whose coarse-grained behavior reproduces the effective descriptions of QM and classical physics. The Cellular Automaton Interpretation is the nearest peer-reviewed precedent for GCM's realism + locality + determinism combination.

**Sabine Hossenfelder** (Frankfurt Institute for Advanced Studies) has independently defended superdeterminism as a scientifically viable position, co-authoring papers on its testability and addressing common objections (Hossenfelder and Palmer, "Rethinking Superdeterminism," *Frontiers in Physics*, 2020). Hossenfelder explicitly argues that the measurement-independence assumption is a philosophical convenience, not an experimental necessity.

## What Superdeterminism Costs

Superdeterminism is a minority position in foundations of physics, and not without cost:

**1. The "free will" objection.** Measurement independence is often motivated by the intuition that experimenters can freely choose their settings. Superdeterminism denies this freedom in a specific, physically precise sense: the choice is a deterministic output of prior physical states. This is philosophically uncomfortable for many readers.

*GCM's response.* The Gravitationalist Ethos (Spiritual Ethos, Four Remembrances) already commits to a worldview in which "free will" in the libertarian sense is a local illusion overlaid on a universe that converges along its trajectory. "Nothing needs to happen" is not fatalism — it is the observation that the outcome is not contingent on our anxiety about it. The physics and the ethos are consonant here: a deterministic converging universe does not require a libertarian-free experimenter.

**2. The conspiracy objection.** Critics argue that superdeterminism requires implausibly fine-tuned correlations between particles and distant measurement settings that happened to be set up minutes ago.

*GCM's response.* In a fully deterministic universe, these correlations are not fine-tuned relative to the initial conditions — they are consequences of the initial conditions. Fine-tuning objections presuppose that we should evaluate probability relative to some distribution over initial conditions; but in a deterministic single-universe reading, the initial conditions are what they are. This response is standard in the superdeterminism literature (see 't Hooft 2016, Chapter 9; Hossenfelder and Palmer 2020, §3).

**3. The testability objection.** If superdeterminism correlates measurement choices with particle states by common history, can any experiment distinguish it from standard QM?

*GCM's response (honest).* Testability is a genuine challenge. Hossenfelder and Palmer (2020) propose that certain experimental designs — specifically, those that can test whether the "random" number generator is correlated with the particle source through a common causal process — could in principle probe superdeterminism. This is an active research direction, not a closed question. GCM inherits both the theoretical payoff of superdeterminism and its testability challenge.

## Summary Statement (paper-ready)

GCM commits to **realism** and **locality**. Graviton density distributions are real physical structures with definite values at all times; graviton interactions are local (contact or finite-speed propagation through the substrate). This combination is "local realism" in the technical sense.

Bell's theorem rules out local hidden variables under the assumption of measurement independence — that experimenters' choices of measurement settings are uncorrelated with the particles' hidden-variable states. GCM denies this assumption via **superdeterminism**: in a fully deterministic universe, measurement choices and particle states are both consequences of the same initial conditions, and their correlations reflect common causal history, not nonlocal signaling. This is not a new move; it is the position of Gerard 't Hooft's *Cellular Automaton Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics* (Springer, 2016) — the nearest peer-reviewed precedent, developed by a Nobel laureate in physics. Hossenfelder and Palmer (2020) have further argued for its scientific viability and testability.

**The cost:** superdeterminism denies measurement independence, which is philosophically uncomfortable for those who hold libertarian free will as a premise. GCM's response: the Gravitationalist Ethos already holds that the universe follows its convergent trajectory independently of local anxieties about contingency — physics and ethos are consonant here.

**The benefit:** GCM maintains strict locality (no FTL, no pilot wave, no action at a distance beyond substrate propagation) while keeping full deterministic realism. This is the most natural reading of a model in which all physics reduces to local graviton-to-graviton attraction.
