# L1 — Caveats

**Claim code:** L1

---

## Caveat 1 — The Value is Not Derived

$m_\gamma \approx 10^{-54}$ kg is an estimate, not a derivation from GCM's framework. GCM's ontology (photon as graviton cluster) implies $m_\gamma > 0$, but gives no mechanism for *why* it should be $10^{-54}$ kg rather than $10^{-60}$ kg or $10^{-50}$ kg. Until GCM specifies how many gravitons a photon contains and what $m_g$ (graviton mass) is independently, the photon mass is a free parameter constrained only by experimental bounds.

**What would make this green:** A derivation from GCM first principles showing $m_\gamma = N_{\text{photon}} \cdot m_g$ where both $N_{\text{photon}}$ and $m_g$ are independently derived, producing a value that matches within experimental bounds.

---

## Caveat 2 — Thin Margin Against Tighter Bounds

The plan document emphasizes the margin against the Wang 2023 FRB bound (2.1 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV/c²), where GCM is ~3,740× below. But tighter photon-mass bounds exist:

- **PDG 2022 combined ($\sim 10^{-18}$ eV/c²):** GCM is only ~1.8× below this bound.
- **Magnetostatic analysis (Ryutov 2007):** Placed constraints in a similar range.
- **Laboratory Coulomb bounds:** In principle can reach $m_\gamma < 10^{-18}$ eV/c² or tighter.

GCM's claimed $5.61 \times 10^{-19}$ eV/c² is below all published bounds as of this writing, but the margin against the tightest bounds is thin — a factor of ~2, not orders of magnitude. Any improvement in experimental sensitivity by a factor of ~2 in the tightest existing bound would exclude GCM's current estimate and require a smaller value.

**Tracking condition:** Check PDG updates annually. If any bound drops below $5 \times 10^{-19}$ eV/c², the claim needs revision.

---

## Caveat 3 — Non-Relativistic Dispersion Concern

A massive photon travels at $v < c$ (see L2). This would produce frequency-dependent group velocities (dispersion in vacuum), which is constrained to extraordinary precision by multi-wavelength observations of distant sources (e.g., gamma-ray bursts observed at different energies arriving simultaneously). The Wang 2023 FRB bound already accounts for this; the photon mass must not produce detectable dispersion.

GCM's claimed mass passes the FRB bound, but dispersion constraints are ongoing and may tighten.

---

## Caveat 4 — U(1) Gauge Symmetry

The standard model's photon is exactly massless because of U(1) electromagnetic gauge symmetry. A massive photon violates this symmetry (Proca field). GCM's framework does not invoke U(1) gauge symmetry — which is the philosophical basis for claiming $m_\gamma > 0$ — but this means GCM must also explain why the standard model's U(1) structure works so well at all accessible energies despite the underlying massive-photon substrate. This is an open theoretical challenge, not addressed in this claim.

---

## What Would Revise or Falsify This Claim

1. Any experimental bound below $5 \times 10^{-19}$ eV/c² would require GCM to revise its photon mass estimate downward.
2. A derivation from GCM showing $m_\gamma$ must equal zero (e.g., if the photon's graviton cluster has a special zero-mass property) would require rethinking the entire L-series.
3. The Wolfram verification (Check 3, currently PENDING) could in principle reveal an arithmetic error in the hand calculation.
